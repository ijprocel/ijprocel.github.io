The STAR method: the perfect way to answer interview questions

We've all been there before: sitting in an interview, knowing we have just the right anecdote for the question at hand, but unable to organize our thoughts. The key to avoid getting tongue-tied is to have a powerful, readily-adaptable framework at hand for turning memories, experiences, and emotions into a well-crafted narrative: the STAR method.

So what exactly is the STAR method? STAR stands for:

  • Situation
  • Task
  • Action
  • Result

When formulating a response to an interview question, you should start with the:

Situation

You should begin by giving the background information needed to understand your contribution. Think big picture. Give your interviewer a sense of what challenges your group or organization was facing, what was at stake if the situation took a turn for the worse and what you stood to gain from resolving the situation successfully.

Task

Whereas the goal when describing the situation was to give a sense of the big picture, now it's time to get more specific. What exactly was your part in the situation at hand? How was that determined? Did you take the initiative when deciding what to do next?

Action

You've described what your task was, now get more specific: How did you carry it out? Did unexpected problems crop up, forcing you to think on your feet? What kinds of skills did you demonstrate when carrying out your task.

Result

The interviewer now knows what happened before and during, but what happened after? If things went very well, how did those around you benefit from your actions? If they didn't go so well, what valuable lessons did you take away from the experience?
