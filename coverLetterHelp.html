Cover Letter Checklist

Writing a cover letter can be a daunting, but potentially rewarding, exercise. A cover letter gives you the opportunity to flesh yourself out for an employer, beyond a resume. You can show off your written communication skills, give some hints about your personality, and really emphasize your best qualities. You can find some wonderful, more comprehensive guides out there, but this is meant to be a quick, easily-digestible checklist that you can quickly review!

We'll start with the...

Heading

First on the page should be your contact info: name, address, phone number, and email. Then, skip a line and write today's date. The next part can be tricky: if possible, you should find out who the hiring manager for the position is and address the letter to them. If you've successfully identified this person, list their contact information next: name, office address, phone number, and email. If you're not sure who the hiring manager is, some sources recommend addressing the letter to a senior HR manager in the company, while others recommend skipping this altogether.

Salutation

If you've identified a recipient, begin your letter with "Dear Ms./Mr. Lastname". If you haven't, use a salutation that is generic, but as specific as possible (Dear (CompanyName) Software Development Hiring Manager, as opposed to Dear Hiring Manager).

End your salutation with a comma and skip a line.

Length

While writing, keep in mind that your cover letter should fit on one page and be a quick read. 250-400 words is a good length.

Intro

The goal of an introduction is to grab the reader's attention. Resist the urge to begin with "I'm writing this letter to express my interest in the position of..." . Try some of these strategies instead.

Main Body

Your goal when writing the main body of your cover letter is to talk about your strengths and achievements in a way that is tailored to the employer and position. In his famous book How to Win Friends and Influence People, Dale Carnegie reminds us to always talk in terms of the other person's interests. When you write the main body of your cover letter, don't just brag about yourself or talk about what you want from the potential employer. Talk about what you can do for them.

Conclusion

Now it's time to wrap things up. Express a desire to further discuss your mutual interests with the reader and thank them for taking the time to review your letter. Sign the letter:

Sincerely,

Your Name
